    CARLSON, P.J.

     

    INTRODUCTION

    [1]            The accused, Lyle Barker-Young is charged with leaving the scene of a traffic accident that resulted in a fatality, on June 6, 2024, in Winnipeg, Manitoba, contrary to s. 320.16(3) of the Criminal Code (the Code).

    [2]            Mr. Barker-Young was not driving at the time of the accident.  He was the front seat passenger in a vehicle driven by his roommate, Beckham Severight, when that vehicle hit the bicycle that Mr. Robbie Jenner was riding. That impact caused injuries to Mr. Jenner that proved fatal.

    [3]            Mr. Severight has already been convicted and sentenced for his dangerous driving causing death and for leaving the scene of the accident.

    [4]            Mr. Barker-Young pleaded not guilty to the s. 320.16(3) offence charged. The matter proceeded to trial.

    THE EVIDENCE

    [5]            A Statement of Agreed Facts was filed.

    [6]            Mr. Barker-Young provided a video and audio statement to police on June 6, 2024. Following a voir dire as to voluntariness, that statement was admitted into the trial evidence.

    [7]            The Crown called three civilian witnesses to testify, and five Winnipeg Police Service (WPS) officers.

    [8]            Mr. Barker-Young called WPS Constable (Cst.) Jack McFadden to testify. Mr. Barker-Young, as is his right, chose not to testify, and did not call any other witnesses.

    The Collision

    [9]            Based on the Statement of Agreed Facts, it is established that:

    •        On June 6, 2024, Mr. Severight and Mr. Barker-Young left the residence they were sharing at 207 – 450 Talbot Avenue, in Winnipeg, Manitoba, in the early morning hours.

    •        They went for a drive in a white 2013 BMW 328i XDrive vehicle (the vehicle) being leased by Mr. Barker-Young.

    •        They drove the vehicle on Wellington Crescent.

    •        Mr. Severight was driving. Mr. Barker-Young was seated in the front passenger seat. No one else was in the vehicle.

    •        The posted speed limit on the section of Wellington Crescent where they were driving was 50 kms per hour.

    •        Just before 7:00 a.m., Mr. Severight was driving the vehicle eastbound on Wellington Crescent, near Cockburn Street.  Wellington Crescent, at that location, is a two-way, four lane street that is paved.

    •        At the same time, Mr. Jenner was riding his bicycle in the right-hand curb lane.  He was wearing reflective clothing and a helmet.

    •        The vehicle approached Mr. Jenner from behind and hit Mr. Jenner’s bicycle.

    •        Mr. Severight drove the vehicle away from the scene of the collision.

    •        Mr. Jenner was pronounced deceased at 7:38 a.m. as a result of multiple blunt force injuries he sustained in the collision with the vehicle.

    •        Mr. Barker-Young was arrested on June 6, 2024, at 3:30 p.m.

    [10]         Two civilians, who happened to be proximate to the scene of the collision on June 6, 2024, were called to testify.

    [11]         Karleigh Danowski was driving to work in her vehicle, westbound on Wellington Crescent, around 7:00 a.m. The weather was clear. She observed a white car travelling eastbound that she thought was going really fast. That white car passed her and then, when she had just passed the intersection of Wellington Crescent and Cockburn Street, she heard a loud bang. She looked in her rear-view mirror and saw the person now known to be Mr. Jenner “fly ten feet into the air and land on the ground” on the boulevard just east of Cockburn Street. The white car did not stop or slow down at all. It kept driving. She pulled her own vehicle over, called 911 and went to the scene to offer assistance. She observed debris on the road at the scene of the collision.

    [12]         Dakeisoma Wokoma started jogging down Wellington Crescent going westbound at 6:53 a.m. on June 6, 2024.  About five minutes later, he heard a loud bang. He turned and looked to the left across the street, saw the person now known to be Mr. Jenner in the air and then land on the ground, and saw a white car speeding off. The white car was going too fast for Mr. Wokoma to get the licence plate. The white car skidded and hit the curb, and did not stop or slow down. Mr. Wokoma gave a statement to an officer on scene. That officer, Cst. Romaniuk, made a sketch in his notebook based on the description Mr. Wokoma gave of the car skidding. That sketch was tendered as an exhibit and Mr. Wokoma confirmed it accurately depicted what he told Cst. Romaniuk about the car skidding and not stopping. Mr. Wokoma called 911 and went to assist, administering CPR and staying with Mr. Jenner until he was removed from the scene. Mr. Wokoma said he has lived in that area for four years and he has never seen a vehicle drive as fast as the white car was driving down Wellington Crescent.

    [13]         Each of these civilian witnesses provided their lay opinion as to the speed of the white car, acknowledging they were not experts in estimating speed. Ms. Danowski estimated it was going 100 kms per hour. Mr. Wokoma estimated it was going at least 120 kms per hour. Mr. Barker-Young, in his statement to police, estimated the speed of the vehicle on Wellington Crescent to be “60, 80, 75 maybe” and acknowledged that “we were going fast, like too fast”.

    [14]         Both Ms. Danowski and Mr. Wokoma said the white vehicle did not stop or slow down after hitting the cyclist, and kept on driving away from the scene.

    [15]         Both Ms. Danowski and Mr. Wokoma gave their evidence in a straightforward way. Their evidence was not shaken in any meaningful way on cross-examination. It was clear that what they observed on June 6, 2024, was traumatic for them, and memorable. I accept their evidence as reliable and credible.

    The vehicle involved in the collision

    [16]         WPS Cst. Bryan Romaniuk gave evidence as to the investigative steps he took to trace the vehicle that was involved in the collision. Cst. Corey Petrash and Cst. Cody Gibson testified that, based on information received from Cst. Romaniuk’s investigation, around 12:20 p.m. on June 6, 2024, they located the vehicle.  It was parked in front of 415 William Newton Avenue, a street to the south of the building that Mr. Barker-Young and Mr. Severight lived in at 450 Talbot Avenue.

    [17]         The Statement of Agreed Facts provides that the vehicle involved in the collision was a white 2013 BMW 328i XDrive 4-door sedan bearing Manitoba Licence Plate LMS 376, and that on June 6, 2024, Mr. Barker-Young was the lessee of the vehicle, having leased it from a numbered company for a week on May 31, 2024.

    [18]         The vehicle was observed shortly after the collision to have a severely damaged windshield. Witness Beverly Cochrane testified that just before 8:00 a.m. on June 6, 2024, she observed a vehicle (that turned out to be the vehicle involved in the collision), driving eastbound in front of her vehicle on Inkster Boulevard, just past Route 90.  She observed some concerning driving by the vehicle, that there were two occupants inside and that the windshield was completely smashed. She called 911 to report the concerning driving, as she thought the driver could be impaired. Cst. Romaniuk followed up on this call, and his investigation that followed led to the identification and location of the vehicle.

    [19]         The vehicle was also observed on video taken from home surveillance at 486 Wardlaw Avenue.  That address is depicted on a map tendered in evidence, to be approximately .8 to .9 of a kilometre from the collision site. That video was tendered as evidence. WPS Cst. Jack McFadden testified that he obtained that video surveillance. Though the timestamp on the video could not be verified, Cst. McFadden had information from the homeowner that the vehicle in the video went by 486 Wardlaw Avenue between 7:00 and 7:15 a.m. on June 6, 2024. Cst. McFadden testified he took a still shot from the video, which showed windshield damage on the driver’s side of the vehicle and separation between the front hood and bumper. The video shows the vehicle going fast, though the speed cannot be determined from the video.

    [20]         Csts. Petrash and Gibson testified that when they located the vehicle in front of 415 William Newton Avenue, they observed extensive damage to the front of the vehicle, primarily on the front driver’s side. There was damage to the front bumper, a dent in the hood and the windshield was heavily damaged. Photos of the vehicle, showing the damage, were tendered as exhibits.

    [21]         None of this evidence as to the vehicle was disputed. I accept the evidence as reliable and credible.

    What happened just before and just after the collision?

    [22]         Mr. Barker-Young’s statement made to police is the only evidence as to what was said between Mr. Severight and Mr. Barker-Young, and what actions were taken by them at the time of, and after the collision with Mr. Jenner. Mr. Barker-Young told police the following:

    •        At the time of the collision, Mr. Severight was driving and Mr. Barker-Young was in the passenger seat.

    •        When the vehicle collided with the cyclist, Mr. Barker-Young told Mr. Severight not to stop the car and to keep driving.

    •        At some point after the collision, Mr. Barker-Young took over driving, ultimately driving them in the vehicle back to their residence on Talbot Avenue and parking the vehicle.

    [23]         Mr. Barker-Young made the following comments to police during his statement:

    •        He was asked “who made the choice to drive away?”. Mr. Barker-Young replied, “I did”.

    •        He was asked “were you scared?” Mr. Barker-Young said, “No. I wasn’t…I wasn’t in the driver’s seat at the time. So it happened and he didn’t know what to do. Told him to keep driving up a bit. We stopped. And I told him to get out, and then we switched but….I just switched ‘cause I knew he couldn’t fuckin drive anymore. And like, I don’t know. But yeah, it was my idea to go”.

    •        Referring to Mr. Severight, he said, “Like he was pretty much tripping out. I just told his ass, like, drive man”.

    •        About the drive and speed, he said, “But the joy ride, cruise, whatever you want to call it, yeah, I don’t know what street you said that happened at but yeah, it was a curved road. And I…we were going too much…like we were going fast, like too fast”.

    Mr. Barker-Young’s Statement

    [24]         Defence counsel says that although the Court found Mr. Barker-Young’s statement given to police to have been made voluntarily, and to be admissible, the Court should have concerns about the reliability of some of the comments made by Mr. Barker-Young to police during his statement. Defence counsel says parts of the statement are unclear, vague and are contradicted by other witnesses, and says that the statement shows that Mr. Barker-Young was confused. Defence points to Mr. Barker-Young telling police he does not know where he and Mr. Severight switched drivers after the collision and saying it could have even been on the curve where the collision happened. The civilian evidence is clear that the vehicle did not stop at all at the scene (which was on the curve of the road) and sped away from the scene. Also, Mr. Barker-Young says, a number of times, that things “took a minute”, when the time elapsed, based on other evidence, was clearly more than a minute.

    [25]         I note that there are a number of things said by Mr. Barker-Young in his statement that are confirmed by other evidence. Examples include:

    •        Mr. Barker-Young told police that after he took over driving and he was trying to get home, they were lost. Indeed the civilian evidence of Ms. Cochrane confirms that when she saw the vehicle around 8:00 a.m., it was at a location that was not close in proximity to Mr. Barker-Young’s residence and was also not in the area that Mr. Barker-Young said he thought they were.

    •        The keys to the vehicle and the rental agreement for the vehicle were located during a search of Mr. Barker-Young’s residence, in the locations Mr. Barker-Young told police they would be.

    •        The details of the vehicle rental agreement as explained by Mr. Barker-Young to police were confirmed by the agreement itself, which was filed as part of the Agreed Statement of Facts.

    •        The information he provided to police about who was driving, and the time of day the collision happened were accurate.

    [26]         In my view, the comments Mr. Barker-Young made to police about the things he said to Mr. Severight about not stopping and to keep driving were very clear and were consistent within his statement. No confusion was apparent in these parts of his statement. He said the same thing in different ways and never provided inconsistent or uncertain information about whose decision it was not to stop the vehicle and that he took over driving because Mr. Severight could no longer drive. A read of Mr. Barker-Young’s complete statement makes it clear that during the first part of the statement, he declined to share any substantive information, but that he then made a decision to explain what happened, confirming he was not driving at the time of collision but that it was his decision not to stop and that he took over driving shortly after the collision. The information provided by Mr. Barker-Young about what he told Mr. Severight and about taking over the driving, I find to be sufficiently reliable, to permit me to make the inference that he told Mr. Severight not to stop the vehicle and to keep driving immediately or almost immediately upon the vehicle colliding with the cyclist. I cannot determine from the evidence exactly when or where Mr. Barker-Young took over the driving, but his statement makes it clear that he did, and that it was soon after the collision.   

    POSITIONS OF THE PARTIES

    [27]         The Crown says the Court should be satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. Barker-Young is guilty of the offence as a principal, pursuant to s. 21(1)(a) of the Code, or in the alternative, as a party pursuant to s. 21(1)(b) and/or (c) of the Code.

    [28]         Mr. Barker-Young says he cannot be convicted of the offence as a principal because he was not operating the vehicle at the time of the collision. He says he cannot be convicted as a principal or as a party because the actus reus of the s. 320.16(3) offence was complete when Mr. Severight failed to stop at the scene, such that the offence was completed before Mr. Barker-Young could have said or done anything to influence Mr. Severight’s conduct. Mr. Barker-Young argues that his own driving, which happened at some point after the collision, cannot be characterized as a continuation of the offence because the actus reus of the offence had already been completed by then.

    [29]         Further, Mr. Barker-Young says since the offence was complete when Mr. Severight continued to drive without stopping at the scene, any words said by Mr. Barker-Young to Mr. Severight to keep driving, or conduct by Mr. Barker-Young, was too late to even be considered as aiding or abetting the commission of the offence, such that Mr. Barker-Young could not be convicted as a party to the offence.

    THE LAW

    [30]          Mr. Barker-Young is charged pursuant to s. 320.16(3) of the Code. Section 320.16 reads as follows:

    320.16 (1) Everyone commits an offence who operates a conveyance and who at the time of operating the conveyance knows that, or is reckless as to whether, the conveyance has been involved in an accident with a person or another conveyance and who fails, without reasonable excuse, to stop the conveyance, give their name and address and, if any person has been injured or appears to require assistance, offer assistance.

    (2) Everyone commits an offence who commits an offence under subsection (1) and who at the time of committing the offence knows that, or is reckless as to whether, the accident resulted in bodily harm to another person.

    (3) Everyone commits an offence who commits an offence under subsection (1) and who, at the time of committing the offence, knows that, or is reckless as to whether, the accident resulted in the death of another person or in bodily harm to another person whose death ensues.

    [31]         It is important to note that the offence of failing to stop at the scene of an accident, and related definitions relating to “operating conveyances”, have evolved over time and been subject to some significant revisions by Parliament. It is fair to say that those changes have expanded the scope of potential criminal liability for failing to stop at the scene of an accident, presumably reflecting Parliament’s commitment to deterring those involved in accidents from not stopping, providing particulars and rendering assistance, especially in cases where someone is hurt or killed.

    [32]         The precursor to s. 320.16 was s. 233(2). Conviction under that section (and earlier versions thereof) required proof of an intent to escape criminal or civil liability. That is not required under s. 320.16.

    [33]         As early as 1953 – 1954, s. 221(2) replaced an earlier section which had limited liability for not stopping at the scene of an accident to a person who was driving at the time of the collision. Section 221(2) changed the wording from that to  “Everyone who, having the care, charge or control of a vehicle…”.

    [34]         Amendments to the Code in 2018 made changes to offences involving conveyances, defined to include motor vehicles (s. 320.11). Section 320.11 defines “operate” in relation to offences under Part VIII.1 of the Code (which includes s. 320.16) as “in respect of a motor vehicle, to drive it or to have care or control of it”. And, at the same time, s. 320.16 was added in its current form, attaching criminality to everyone who “operates a conveyance” and meets the other requirements of the section.

    [35]         In R. v. Boudreault, 2012 SCC 56 (CanLII), [2012] 3 S.C.R. 157, at para. 66, it was stated that the words “care or control”:

    “are not technical legal words in this context.  The ordinary sense of the word ‘care’ denotes charge or protection while ‘control’ denotes command or direction…It follows that if the ordinary meaning of these words applies, one is in care or control of a motor vehicle when one acts to assume the present ability to operate the motor vehicle or has its superintendence or management”.

     

    [36]         It is not solely a driver that can be guilty of a s. 320.16(3) offence.  An “operator” of a conveyance (someone who has care or control of the conveyance, even if they are not driving at the time of the accident) may be guilty. That is clear by wording of s. 320.11 and s. 320.16. Further, caselaw has confirmed that persons other than drivers have been found to be operating a vehicle by having care or control, and have been convicted of failing to stop at the scene of a collision. Those cases include R. v. Pierre, 1998 Carswell 2239 (Ontario Court of Justice – General Division); Saletes v. R., 1985 3 (Quebec Superior Court); and R. v. Addley, 2014 ONCJ 773.

    [37]         Someone who takes over driving after a collision, and after the person who was driving at the time of the collision has failed to stop, may be guilty of a failing to stop offence, on the basis they assumed care or control of the vehicle. In the Pierre case, a person who took over driving after a collision occurred when another person was driving, was found guilty of the failing to stop offence.

    [38]          Whether a driver and a non driver who was operating a vehicle by exercising care or control, can both be guilty of a failing to stop offence related to the same accident, as principals, is not answered definitively in the caselaw to date. 

    [39]         In R. v. Slessor, 1969 CanLII 248 (ON CA), [1970] 1 O.R. 664 (ONCA), C.J. Gale queried, in obiter, “Can a driver and a passenger both be guilty under s. 221(2) simultaneously on the same facts?” and then said “I think not”. He said:

    Apart from joint ventures, I should think that only one person in the car would have care, charge or control and that if this person is a passenger he must have a degree of exercisable and exercised control over the actual driver. 

     

    [40]         In my view, s. 320.16(3) does not necessarily preclude more than one person being guilty of that offence relative to the same accident. The facts pertaining to a driver may be different than the facts pertaining to a passenger/operator, even where the same accident is involved. Whether a particular person is guilty will depend on an analysis as to whether that person can, on the facts relating to that person alone, be found to have committed the actus reus and to have the requisite mens rea to have committed the offence as principal.  

    ANALYSIS

    [41]         Mr. Severight and Mr. Barker-Young were both charged with a s. 320.16(3) offence. Both charges arose from the same collision.

    [42]         Mr. Barker-Young relies on the case of R. v. TLC, 2024 ABCJ (Alberta Court of Justice) to say that the actus reus of the offence crystallized as soon as Mr. Severight failed to perform any of the three acts required by s. 320.16, that is stopping, providing particulars and rendering assistance. That analysis would apply to Mr. Severight’s commission of the offence.  

    [43]         A separate consideration is required to determine if Mr. Barker-Young committed the actus reus and had the mens rea required to be guilty of the offence. Mr. Barker-Young’s potential offending is not necessarily the same as Mr. Severight’s offending.

    [44]         Just because Mr. Severight’s s. 320.16 offending crystallized on, or very shortly after the collision when he kept driving and sped away without stopping, that does not necessarily mean that Mr. Barker-Young was precluded from committing his own fail to stop offence.

    [45]         Mr. Barker-Young relies on C.J. Gale’s query in obiter in the Slessor case as to whether a driver and passenger could both be guilty of a failing to stop offence on the same facts.  In my view, in this case, the facts relating to Mr. Severight and Mr. Barker-Young are not the same. Mr. Severight was driving, hit the cyclist and did not stop. Mr. Barker-Young, on impact or shortly after that, took over control of the vehicle by directing Mr. Severight not to stop and to keep driving, then took over driving himself, and failed to stop.

    [46]         It is the facts relating to Mr. Barker-Young’s actions and words that require analysis as to whether Mr. Barker-Young committed the actus reus and had the mens rea to commit the s. 320.16 offence.

    Elements of the “failure to stop” offence

    [47]         To convict Mr. Barker-Young as a principal, I need to be persuaded beyond a reasonable doubt that he committed the actus reus of the s. 320.16(3) offence and that he had the requisite mens rea to commit it.

    [48]         It has been proven beyond a reasonable doubt that the vehicle Mr. Barker-Young was in collided with Mr. Jenner’s bicycle and that Mr. Jenner was killed as a result of injuries sustained in that collision.

    [49]         The requisite actus reus is that Mr. Barker-Young:

    1.     Operated the conveyance (that is, that he drove the vehicle or otherwise had “care or control” of the vehicle) that was involved in the collision with Mr. Jenner’s bicycle; and

    2.     Failed to stop the conveyance without reasonable excuse, provide his name and address, and offer assistance.

    [50]         No excuse has been put forward for consideration as to the reasonableness of not stopping.

    [51]         The requisite mens rea that Mr. Barker-Young must have had at the time he committed the actus reus was:

    1.     That the conveyance had been in an accident with the cyclist; and

    2.     That he had knowledge or was reckless that the accident resulted in the death of the cyclist or in bodily harm to the cyclist and death ensued.

    Did Mr. Barker-Young commit the requisite actus reus?

    [52]         There are two actions Mr. Barker-Young took that lead me to conclude he committed the actus reus of the s. 320.16(3) offence.

    [53]         First, he assumed “operation” of the vehicle at the time of, or immediately after, the collision by directing Mr. Severight to keep driving, which direction Mr. Severight followed.

    [54]         Second, he took over the driving shortly after the collision.

    [55]         And then, Mr. Barker-Young failed to stop, provide particulars and render assistance.

    [56]         Although I am not satisfied the evidence supports that Mr. Barker-Young had care or control of the vehicle during the entire time Mr. Severight was driving, Mr. Barker-Young’s clear admissions in his statement to police that it was his decision not to stop, and that he told Mr. Severight to keep driving when the collision happened, and Mr. Severight’s compliance with Mr. Barker-Young’s direction to keep going (when, according to Mr. Barker-Young, Mr. Severight did not know what to do), make it clear that, when Mr. Barker-Young gave that direction to Mr. Severight, Mr. Barker-Young had assumed control of the vehicle such that he was then “operating” it. Mr. Barker-Young was exercising control at the time and Mr. Severight was following that direction. This is similar to the situation in the Pierre case, in which the accused, the owner of a vehicle who was a passenger when a collision with a pedestrian occurred, told the driver to take the vehicle “off the road”, after the collision happened, which the driver did.  The accused owner/passenger then took over driving. The accused owner/passenger in Pierre was convicted of the failing to stop offence (then, s. 252 of the Code) on the basis that she took over operation of the vehicle by her direction to the driver.

    [57]         Mr. Barker-Young’s position that the fail to stop offence was complete essentially immediately on the vehicle hitting the cyclist and not stopping, in my view, imposes a narrow and artificial time limit on the decision not to stop that is not present or contemplated by the section. Indeed, in Pierre, that accused’s actions in leaving the scene without providing particulars or assistance happened after the car driven by the original driver had hit the pedestrian, the driver had actually stopped the car at the scene though had not provided particulars or rendered assistance, and then left the scene. In Pierre, the Court did not impose the kind of narrow timeline required to assess the elements of the offence that Mr. Barker-Young says is required.

    [58]         Further, I am satisfied that once Mr. Barker-Young took over driving of the vehicle, at his own insistence, his actions fit within the actus reus of the wording of the charged offence. In his statement, Mr. Barker-Young’s comments are consistent with him getting into the driver’s seat a short distance from the collision site. None of the surveillance evidence is inconsistent with that assertion. Once he took over driving, he could have returned to the scene, stopped, provided particulars and rendered assistance. He did not. There is no reason that Parliament would not have intended such a scenario to fall within the section. Parliament could easily have used wording in s. 320.16 requiring the “operation” of the conveyance to be limited to operation at the time of the collision.  It did not so limit the section. The rationale and public policy underlying the section surely is to ensure that help is obtained as soon as possible for someone who is hurt in a traffic accident, and also to ensure that particulars are provided for any potential civil and/or criminal investigation. There is no rationale for limiting the timeframe of commission of the s. 320.16 offence to the instant the collision happens.

    [59]         I am satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. Barker-Young committed the actus reus of the s. 320.16(3) offence as a principal.

    Did Mr. Barker-Young have the requisite mens rea?

    [60]         Both at the time Mr. Barker-Young took over care or control of the vehicle (by verbal direction to Mr. Severight to keep driving) and once he had actually taken over the driving, Mr. Barker-Young was aware the vehicle had been in an accident with the cyclist. He was there. He saw it happen. He confirmed that in his statement when he told the police “I wasn’t driving that car when we hit the biker”.

    [61]         Did Mr. Barker-Young have knowledge or was he reckless that the accident resulted in the death of the cyclist or in bodily harm to the cyclist and death ensued?

    [62]         In his statement to police, Mr. Barker-Young made comments as to what he was thinking at the time of the collision:

    •        “Like, I don’t know why I kept going. I didn’t…honestly, I just told myself that I didn’t think he could have got hurt.  But obviously a part of myself kind of figured he could have too, but you just hope that he didn’t right?”

    •        “The windshield was really fucked up…”.

    [63]         Knowledge can be proven by either actual knowledge or by imputed knowledge which includes wilful blindness (R. v. Edwards, 2020 BCCA 253 at para. 95). Wilful blindness is equivalent to actual knowledge (R. v. Briscoe, 2010 SCC 13).

    [64]         In R. v. Morrison, 2019 SCC 15, Justice Moldaver stated at para. 98 that:

    Wilful blindness exists where an accused’s “suspicion is aroused to the point where he or she sees the need for further inquiries, but deliberately chooses not to make those inquiries” (R. v. Briscoe, 2010 SCC 13 at para 21…).   Wilful blindness has been characterized as “deliberate ignorance” because it connotes an actual process of supressing a suspicion”: D. Stuart, Canadian Criminal Law: A Treatise (7th ed. 2014) at p. 261).  

     

    [65]         Recklessness is a lower standard than wilful blindness and requires “…knowledge of a danger or risk and persistence in a course of conduct which creates a risk that the prohibited result will occur…” (Briscoe, para. 22, citing R. v. Sansregret, 1985 CanLII 79 (SCC), [1985] 1 S.C.R. 570 at p. 584).

    [66]         The comments Mr. Barker-Young made in his statement, supplemented by the independent evidence about the speed the vehicle was going, that there was a loud bang on impact of the vehicle with the cyclist, that Mr. Jenner “flew ten feet in the air”, that the windshield of the vehicle was heavily damaged and there was other damage to the front of the vehicle, support that Mr. Barker-Young would have known this was a violent collision that must have resulted in at least bodily harm to the cyclist. He knew the vehicle hit the cyclist and that the vehicle was going “too fast”, in his own words, at the time. I find Mr. Barker-Young was wilfully blind to the fact the cyclist had suffered bodily harm or worse.

    [67]         I am satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. Barker-Young had the requisite mens rea by being wilfully blind to the fact the collision had caused the cyclist at least bodily harm. If he was not wilfully blind, he was at the very least reckless that the collision had caused at least bodily harm to the cyclist.

    CONCLUSION AS TO COMMISSION OF OFFENCE AS A PRINCIPAL

    [68]         For the foregoing reasons, I am satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. Barker-Young committed the actus reus, and had the mens rea required by s. 320.16(3) of the Code. Accordingly, I find Mr. Barker-Young guilty as a principal, in accordance with s. 21(1) of the Code, of the offence charged.

    IN THE ALTERNATIVE, IS MR. BARKER-YOUNG GUILTY AS A PARTY?

     

    [69]         In the event I am mistaken that Mr. Barker-Young is guilty of the offence as a principal, I consider whether he was a party to the s. 320.16(3) offence by virtue of s. 21(b) and/or (c) of the Code, that is by aiding and/or abetting Mr. Severight in committing the fail to stop offence.

    [70]         The actus reus of aiding or abetting is to do (or to omit to do) something that assists or encourages the perpetrator to commit the offence. “Aiding” means to assist or help the perpetrator. Abetting includes “encouraging, instigating, promoting or procuring the crime to be committed” (R. v. Greyeyes, [1997] 2 S.C.R. 285 at para. 26; Briscoe at para. 14). In this case, based on Mr. Barker-Young’s own statement, he told the driver not to stop and to keep driving. The aider or abettor must also have the necessary mens rea. The person must have rendered the assistance for the purpose of aiding the principal offender to commit the crime and knowing that the perpetrator intends to commit the crime.

    [71]         Some caselaw was filed in which a person other than a driver has been convicted as a party to driving offences.

    [72]         In R. v. Kulbacki, 1965 CarswellMan41 (MBCA), a 20-year-old owner of a vehicle was the passenger in a vehicle being driven by a 16-year-old. The driver drove at high speeds. The Court convicted the owner/passenger of dangerous driving as a party pursuant to s. 21(1) of the Code and liable to the same extent as if he had actually been driving the vehicle, on the basis he had aided and abetted the commission of the offence, by doing and/or saying nothing to stop, prevent or trying to stop or prevent the driver from driving at high speeds.

    [73]         In R. v. Akapew, 2009 SKCA 137, an accused was convicted of flight from a peace officer causing death and causing bodily harm. He was a passenger in a stolen truck. An accomplice was driving. When a police officer approached the truck and ordered the occupants to stop, the accused said to the driver “cops, go, go, go”. The driver accelerated the vehicle, hit the officer, causing injury to the officer. The accused passenger was convicted as a party by common intent on the basis his conduct constituted aiding and abetting under s. 21(1)(b) and (c). It was found he had formed a common intention to evade police. The Court of Appeal upheld the trial judge’s reasoning.

    [74]         In R. v. R.(M.), 2011 ONCA 190, an accused was charged with criminal negligence causing death while street racing. The accused was the “flag man”, which entailed dropping his jacket to start a street race in which a participant was killed. At trial, the accused was acquitted. The Crown’s appeal was allowed and a new trial ordered on the basis that the trial judge did not properly consider the accused’s potential liability as a party under s. 21(1) of the Code.

    [75]         In the Saletes case, previously referred to, in which a passenger was found to be guilty as a principal for failing to stop, the judge said if he was wrong to convict the accused as a principal, in the alternative, the accused would have been convicted as an aider and abettor.

    [76]         Mr. Barker-Young’s statement is clear as to what he said to Mr. Severight and the actions each of them took.  He says in several different ways that he told Mr. Severight to keep driving. He said that Mr. Severight could not continue driving so he took over driving. He would have known that neither he nor Mr. Severight would be stopping at the scene, providing particulars or rendering assistance. These aspects of Mr. Barker-Young’s statement are clear and consistent and I have found them reliable.

    [77]         If I am mistaken in convicting Mr. Barker-Young for committing the s. 320.16(3) offence as a principal, I do not hesitate to find I am persuaded beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. Barker-Young at least aided and abetted Mr. Severight in committing the offence of failing to stop at the scene by telling him to keep driving and not to stop, and then taking over the driving and continuing to leave the scene without stopping, providing particulars or rendering assistance.

    DECISION

    [78]         I find Mr. Barker-Young guilty as charged.
